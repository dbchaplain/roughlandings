Rough Landings Day 2: Finding Happiness When...

“God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, courage to change the things I can, and wisdom know the difference”

My freshman year was a whirlwind. I learned a lot and grew the most as a person I ever have. The reason I grew was because life sucked.

When you already have the best anything else feels like second hand. That’s how college track felt. I took the support system I had developed over four years in high school for granted. It was like I moved out from my parents’ house to a new family and suddenly I was supposed to believe, and trust, and enjoy, and respect, and listen to my new family.

I tried, but I expected to find the track family I had at home and that was not realistic. The relationships I had before weren’t made in a month, some not even a year. They were developed and fortified over time. How could I find happiness when my security was gone? Falsely judging my relationships left me with a sense of unease. That self-doubt festered and led to a downward spiral.

The result was an inner battle. I tried to fit myself into my new family. I tried to be a contributing, helpful family member but I could not keep up. Even with their help I didn’t feel good enough. This feeling translated over many different parts of my life. I broke down. I wanted to give up, to quit. I sort of did. After track ended I didn’t even want to look at a pole. My silver lining had become ugly. My therapy had become my enemy. Pole vault was no longer fun, it was work.

How did I pull out of this downward spiral? I started by taking a break. I thought a lot and sought the wisdom to decide if I should quit or continue. After two and a half months of contemplation and unhappiness I decided to practice with my high school coach. It wasn’t until that day that I knew my decision was to stay. I remembered again why I decided to compete at the college level. Being back in the familiar atmosphere, with familiar people allowed me to have fun again. I realized that all the external pressures didn’t matter. I loved my sport and I finally had the wisdom to know what I could change.

I couldn’t change situation, but I could change my attitude. I committed to being happy and having fun. When I made that decision, I found the steady base I needed. I celebrated the differences between my past and present team and while that road was sometimes bumpy it led to where I am now. I look forward to practice again and I’m excited to compete. I’m happy to be doing what I am doing and subsequently I am doing better.

Fun seems like such a basic element but it is so crucial for success in anything you do. Find the fun, and happiness will follow. If you can’t, make a change.
